1. Introduction to Cancer

Definition

Cancer is a group of diseases characterized by uncontrolled, abnormal cell growth. It is a leading global cause of death.

Key Statistics (2020)

  • Approximately 10 million annual fatalities.
  • Over 19 million new cases recorded globally.

To understand cancer, we must examine its diverse biological mechanisms, risk factors, modern diagnostic methods, and tailored treatments.

Cell Development & Mechanism

Figure 1: Comparison between normal cell development and abnormal cancerous growth.

How Cancer Starts

  • Human body is made of trillions of cells that regenerate regularly.
  • When cells grow old or damaged, they die and are replaced.
  • In cancer, this orderly process breaks down.
  • Abnormal cells grow when they shouldn’t and form tumors.

Most Common Types of Cancer

Figure 2: Illustration showing common types of cancer and their locations in the body.

🔷 Carcinomas

Most common type. Originates in epithelial cells (Lung, Breast, Prostate, Colon).

🦴 Sarcomas

Start in connective tissues like fat, muscle, cartilage, and bone.

🩸 Leukemias

Cancers of white blood cells beginning in bone marrow; no solid tumors.

🛡️ Lymphomas

Start in immune system cells (lymphocytes).

Causes & Risk Factors

Figure 3: Major causes including genetic, environmental, and lifestyle factors.

🧬 Genetic Factors

Family history increases risk significantly.

🚬 Lifestyle

Smoking, alcohol consumption, and poor diet are major contributors.

🏭 Environmental

Exposure to asbestos, pesticides, radon, and UV radiation.

🦠 Infections

Certain viruses (HPV, Hepatitis) and bacteria can lead to cancer.

Symptoms & Diagnosis

Figure 4: Common symptoms and diagnostic procedures.

Common Symptoms

  • Persistent fatigue & weight changes
  • Lumps or thickening under skin
  • Skin changes (yellowing, darkening)
  • Persistent cough or bleeding

Diagnostic Tests

  • Physical Exam: Checking for lumps.
  • Lab Tests: Blood and urine analysis.
  • Imaging: CT scans, MRI, X-rays.
  • Biopsy: Definitive cell sample testing.

Complications

🤒 Physical Symptoms

Severe fatigue, pain, nausea/vomiting, hair loss (alopecia).

🔄 Systemic Issues

Infections (neutropenia), anemia, muscle wasting.

🫀 Organ Damage

Lymphedema, bowel obstruction, urinary problems.

🧠 Neurological

"Chemo brain" (cognitive changes), peripheral neuropathy, dizziness.

Treatment Options

🔪 Surgery

Removes the tumor from the body.

💊 Chemotherapy

Drugs to kill fast-growing cells.

☢️ Radiation

High-energy rays to destroy cells.

🛡️ Immunotherapy

Boosts immune system to fight cancer.

🎯 Targeted Therapy

Targets specific gene changes.

⚖️ Hormone Therapy

Lowers hormone levels to stop growth.

Management & Nursing Care

Symptom Management

Managing fatigue, pain, nausea via pharmacological and non-pharmacological methods.

Infection Control

Neutropenic precautions, hand hygiene, monitoring WBC counts.

Treatment Support

Caring for chemo/radiation side effects and surgical sites.

Psychosocial Support

Emotional assistance, education to reduce anxiety.

Nutrition

Monitoring weight and addressing oral issues hindering eating.

Education

Teaching about disease process, meds, and emergency signs.

Conclusion

  • Cancer is a complex group of diseases driven by uncontrolled cell growth.
  • Largely preventable through lifestyle changes and early screening.
  • Advances in molecular biology have improved survival rates.
  • Treatment is shifting toward targeted, personalized therapies.
  • A comprehensive approach combining prevention, early detection, and palliative care is essential.
